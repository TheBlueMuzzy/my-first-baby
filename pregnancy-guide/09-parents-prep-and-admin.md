# Parents' Prep & Admin

The medical files cover the baby. This file covers the parents: your own health, insurance, time off work, money, legal paperwork, and the conversations you should have before the baby arrives. Much of this is easy to forget because no one's measuring a heartbeat at these appointments — but the deadlines here are real, and some (like adding the baby to insurance) have hard cutoffs.

Location context: NYC / New York State. State leave and disability programs below are New York–specific; confirm exact eligibility, amounts, and deadlines with your own HR and insurer, since they change yearly.

## Parents' own health (start now)

### Dad / partner vaccines ("cocooning" — protect the baby through yourself)
- [ ] **Tdap** (whooping cough/pertussis): get one if you haven't had a Tdap booster in ~10 years. Whooping cough is dangerous to newborns, and the baby can't be vaccinated until 2 months — so the adults around the baby being current is the protection. Ideally before baby arrives.
- [ ] **Flu shot** (in season): both parents.
- [ ] **COVID**: stay current per CDC.
- [ ] **Confirm MMR (measles/mumps/rubella) and varicella (chickenpox) immunity**: if you're unsure whether you're immune, a doctor can check with a blood test or just re-vaccinate. (Mom can't get these live vaccines *during* pregnancy, so any adults around the baby being immune matters.)

### Mom's vaccines (handled in the pregnancy files, listed here so it's all in one place)
- Flu and COVID: any trimester (see `02`).
- Tdap: every pregnancy, ~wk 27–36 (see `03`).
- RSV (Abrysvo): seasonal, ~wk 32–36 — or baby gets the antibody after birth instead (see `03`/`06`).

### Both parents
- [ ] **Dental checkup + cleaning.** Safe and recommended in pregnancy; gum health is linked to pregnancy outcomes. Easy to skip, worth doing.
- [ ] **Mental-health baseline.** Line up support *before* you need it — know who you'd call, whether therapy is covered, and that postpartum depression hits dads too (see `06`). Don't wait for a crisis to find a provider.
- [ ] **Dad: get your own physical / be healthy.** You're about to be the engine of the household for a while. Sleep, basic fitness, and not getting sick are part of the prep.

## Insurance (this is where the hidden money is)

- [ ] **Confirm your OB and chosen hospital are in-network.** Also check the anesthesiologist/anesthesia group at that hospital — out-of-network anesthesia is a classic surprise bill.
- [ ] **Read your maternity coverage:** deductible, out-of-pocket maximum, copays, and what "delivery" actually costs you. Call the insurer and ask for an estimate for a vaginal birth *and* a C-section.
- [ ] **Tell your insurer you're pregnant** if your plan requires notification or has a maternity program (some give free perks).
- [ ] **Free breast pump.** Most US insurance plans cover one at no cost — find out the process and timing (some let you order in the third trimester).
- [ ] **FSA/HSA planning.** If you have one, plan contributions around the birth year — a lot of these expenses are eligible. If open enrollment falls before the birth, revisit your elections.
- [ ] **HARD DEADLINE — add the baby to insurance after birth.** Birth is a "qualifying life event," but you typically have only **~30 days** (some plans 60) to add the newborn, or you wait until open enrollment. Know your exact window *now* and put a reminder for the week after the due date. The baby's own coverage matters for the newborn checkup at ~3–5 days.
- [ ] **Decide on cord blood banking** (optional, costs money, has a deadline — you arrange it before birth or not at all). Most families skip private banking; public donation is free where available. Just make it a deliberate choice, not a missed one.

## Time off work (both parents — start reading policies in the 2nd trimester)

Three different things stack together in New York. Don't assume one covers everything.

- [ ] **FMLA (federal job protection):** up to 12 weeks of *unpaid*, job-protected leave — *if* eligible (employer has 50+ employees, you've worked there 12+ months and 1,250+ hours). This protects your job; it doesn't pay you.
- [ ] **NY Short-Term Disability (DBL) — birthing parent:** covers part of mom's income during the medical recovery from birth (typically ~6 weeks vaginal / ~8 weeks C-section). Low weekly cap — check the current amount.
- [ ] **NY Paid Family Leave (PFL) — both parents:** paid bonding leave (up to 12 weeks, at a percentage of your wage up to a cap). **Both** parents can take it; partners often take it *after* mom's disability period to extend coverage at home. Confirm current weeks and pay rate with HR.
- [ ] **Your employer's own parental-leave policy:** read it, get it *in writing*, and find out how it stacks with the state programs above (some employers top up pay; some run concurrently).
- [ ] **Plan the announcement timing at work** and who you tell first. No legal obligation to announce early.
- [ ] **Coordinate the two leaves on a calendar.** Common pattern: overlap for the first stretch, then stagger so someone's home as long as possible before childcare starts.
- [ ] **Budget for the income dip** during leave (see money section).

## Money & budget

- [ ] **Build/refresh an emergency fund** before the income dip from leave.
- [ ] **Estimate the new recurring costs:** diapers, wipes, formula (if used), gear, and — the big NYC one — **childcare/daycare**, which is expensive and has long waitlists. Tour and waitlist early (also noted in `02`).
- [ ] **Sketch a post-baby monthly budget** with the leave-period income factored in.
- [ ] **Big one-time purchases:** car seat (required), crib, stroller — research safety first, register your gear for recall notices.

## Legal & admin

- [ ] **Make/update a will** and **name a legal guardian** for the baby. This is the single most-skipped item and the most important — it decides who raises your child if something happens to both of you.
- [ ] **Life insurance** for both parents (income earner *and* the at-home parent, whose labor would be expensive to replace).
- [ ] **Update beneficiaries** on retirement accounts and existing policies.
- [ ] **Healthcare proxy / medical power of attorney** for mom around the birth (who speaks for her if she can't).
- [ ] **At the hospital:** birth certificate application + Social Security number for the baby (handled there; also in `06`). Bring IDs.
- [ ] **Choose a pediatrician before delivery** — the hospital needs the name at discharge (see `02`/`06`).

## The conversations to have (before the baby, while you can think)

Sit down together and actually decide, rather than colliding on these at 3 a.m. later:
- [ ] **Feeding:** breast, formula, or both — and that the plan can change without it being a failure.
- [ ] **Sleep approach** and how you'll split night wakings (see `07`).
- [ ] **Visitors:** who, when, and the gatekeeping rules for the first weeks (see `06`).
- [ ] **Division of labor** at home during leave and after.
- [ ] **Values:** names, traditions, religion/culture, screen time, photos-on-social-media policy.
- [ ] **Your relationship:** how you'll check in with each other when you're both exhausted.
- [ ] **Support network:** who can bring meals, hold the baby so you nap, and be on call.

## Suggested timing (so nothing piles up at the end)

- **Now → 1st trimester:** dad's Tdap/vaccines, dental, confirm in-network OB & hospital, read insurance basics, start the values conversations.
- **2nd trimester:** read all leave policies and put leave dates on the calendar; start the will/guardianship and life-insurance process; tour daycares/waitlist; order the breast pump if allowed; budget pass.
- **3rd trimester:** finalize leaves with HR, confirm the insurance "add the baby" deadline, choose the pediatrician, set the post-birth reminder to add baby to insurance.
- **After birth (tight deadlines):** add baby to insurance (~30 days), birth certificate + SSN, finalize beneficiaries/guardianship paperwork.

> Reference only. Your HR, insurer, attorney, and doctors override anything here. New York leave/disability rules and dollar amounts change yearly — verify current figures before relying on them.
